We exploit the truncated singular value decomposition and the recently proposed circulant decomposition for an efficient first-order approximation of the multiplication of large dense matrices. A decomposition of each matrix into a sum of a sparse matrix with relatively few dominant entries and a dense residue can also use the above approach. We present methods for multiplication using a Fourier decomposition and a cycle decomposition-based sparsifications. The proposed methods scale favorably in arithmetic operations for square matrices for usable tolerances in relative error. Note that different decompositions for the two matrices in the product are also possible in this approach. Using efficient evaluations for suitability can improve further on the error tolerances demonstrated here. Reducing the scaling of arithmetic operations required in multiplying two dense matrices can widely increase the efficiency of computing. Even though the asymptotic scaling of certain theoretical algorithms is attractive compared to the naive approach, they have been non-trivial in practical implementation. As an alternative, randomized algorithms for approximation have been proposed to incur lower computing effort at the cost of lower precision in the evaluated matrix product. Nevertheless, these methods are not sufficiently precise or efficient to have a wide impact on practical computing. We propose approximations based on relatively few components of a decomposition that each multiply a general matrix efficiently. This allows a first-order approximation of the product of two truncated decompositions. One may also note that in the more recent applications such as the domain of deep learning, multiplication of large matrices is unavoidable. For example, the forward pass of a convolutional neural network processes data in batches and it may perform evaluations involving the weights of the connections. Backpropagation computes the gradient of the loss functions using the chain rule which may again involve the multiplication of large matrices. Computation of self-attention in transformers uses the product of query, key, and value matrices. Such multiplication of large matrices is not restricted to the above cases. In the proposed approach, we first partially decompose a given matrix into a sum of dominant components, and a residue representing the remaining components. We may multiply the dominant components of the partial decompositions with each other as a zeroth order approximation. We can also multiply the residue matrices with those multiplication-friendly components resulting in a first order approximation. The first-order approximation may increase the rank of resulting matrices in many cases, compared to the zeroth order multiplication. This is especially true when using singular value decomposition components where the ranks are one. The performance of this approach in reducing the error is more significant than the increase in rank. The expected relative error in the approximation is shown to be a product of the relative errors in the two matrices. This holds true for distributions of independent entries in the matrices, with a negligible variance. Thus it can be estimated cheaply in arithmetic operations before computing the approximation of multiplication. Hence a low truncation error in the decomposition of one of the two matrices would suffice for a low error in the approximated product.
